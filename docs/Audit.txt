## SECTION 4 — READ MY CURRENT RESULTS LIKE A REFEREE

### What is already convincing

The convincing fact is **not** the huge contemporaneous t-statistics. The convincing fact is that your main LM uncertainty measure stays positive in lagged specifications. For CEO Q&A uncertainty, PRisk remains positive at (t-1) ((3.56\times10^{-5},\ t=2.89,\ p=0.0038)) and (t-2) ((2.77\times10^{-5},\ t=2.24,\ p=0.0249)). For manager Q&A uncertainty, the lagged results are stronger: (4.31\times10^{-5}) at (t-1) ((t=5.27)) and (3.84\times10^{-5}) at (t-2) ((t=4.58)).

The same is true in presentations. CEO presentation uncertainty stays positive at (t-1) ((7.52\times10^{-5},\ t=6.22)) and (t-2) ((4.51\times10^{-5},\ t=3.57)); manager presentation uncertainty also stays positive at both lags ((5.80\times10^{-5},\ t=6.72;\ 2.90\times10^{-5},\ t=3.39)). That matters because it shows the signal is not disappearing as soon as you leave the contemporaneous call.

Your control structure is also directionally serious, not amateur. In the Q&A regressions, analyst-question uncertainty and presentation uncertainty are both strongly positive, which is exactly what Dzielinski’s logic would lead us to expect: answers reflect both the call’s current conditions and the wording of the questions. In your CEO Q&A model, Analyst_QA_Uncertainty is 0.0378 and CEO presentation uncertainty is 0.1048; in the manager Q&A model, the corresponding coefficients are 0.0342 and 0.1080.

### What is only suggestive

The persistence from (t-1) to (t-2) is encouraging, but without lead tests it is still only suggestive. Persistent political risk and persistent communication patterns can produce positive lagged coefficients even without a clean directional relation.

The presentation vs Q&A split is also only suggestive unless you use it carefully. Dzielinski et al. use that split to isolate **style**: presentation is more scripted and firm-culture-like, Q&A more improvised and manager-specific. That does **not** mean your Q&A results identify manager beliefs or style. It only means the split is useful for interpretation.

### What is vulnerable

Your contemporaneous PRisk results are vulnerable. Very vulnerable. Hassan’s PRisk is transcript-based political risk from conference calls, and your dependent variable is transcript-based uncertainty language. In the same quarter, on the same family of documents, large t-statistics can reflect shared information, shared topic salience, and direct lexical overlap. A committee will see that immediately.

Your current time effects also look too weak. The tables show **17 time periods**, which means you are apparently using year fixed effects, not quarter or year-quarter fixed effects, despite working with a firm-quarter/call-level panel from 2002–2018. Political risk and conference-call language move faster than annual frequency. This is a mandatory fix.

### Which coefficients and patterns should be the centerpiece

The centerpiece should be **lagged PRisk predicting the main LM uncertainty measure in Q&A speech**, with manager-level Q&A first and CEO Q&A second. The manager Q&A lagged results are your sharpest combination of power and stability; the CEO Q&A lagged results show the same sign with less precision.

Presentation results should be in the main text only as corroboration, not as the flagship. They are strong, but Dzielinski’s whole point is that presentations load more on scripted firm-level disclosure, while Q&A is the more natural place to look for manager-level language variation.

Weak modal measures should not be central. Contemporaneously they are significant, but the lagged evidence is materially weaker: CEO weak modal at (t-2) is only marginal ((p=0.081)), manager weak modal is null at (t-1) and only significant at (t-2). That is appendix material, not thesis-defining evidence.

Main text:

1. One baseline table with contemporaneous results shown briefly as descriptive evidence.
2. One flagship table with lagged PRisk and the main LM uncertainty outcomes.
3. One timing/falsification table with leads and horse-races.

Yes. Unambiguously yes.

Contemporaneous PRisk belongs in the paper, but as **descriptive support**, not as the thesis-defining estimate.

## SECTION 7 — PRIORITY-RANKED TESTS WITH EXACT LOGIC

### 1. Replace contemporaneous PRisk with lagged PRisk as the main specification

**Purpose:** reduce same-call contamination and sharpen interpretation.
**Threat addressed:** shared transcript source, simultaneity.
**Specification:**
[
Uncertainty_{i,t} = \beta PRisk_{i,t-1} + \Gamma Controls_{i,t} + \alpha_i + \tau_t + \varepsilon_{i,t}
]
with firm FE and **calendar-quarter or year-quarter FE**, not just year FE.
**Why needed here:** your current contemporaneous (t) design is the most attackable part of the thesis because both sides are transcript-based.
**Expected pattern:** (\beta>0), smaller than contemporaneous, but still statistically meaningful.
**Pass:** positive lagged coefficient survives with stronger time FE.
**Fail:** lagged coefficient dies and only contemporaneous remains. Then your thesis becomes mostly a same-call descriptive correlation.
**Effort:** low.
**Placement:** main text.

### 2. Lead test / future-PRisk falsification

**Purpose:** test whether the lagged relation is directional rather than just persistence or omitted trends.
**Threat addressed:** reverse causality, persistence.
**Specification:** regress (Uncertainty_{i,t}) on (PRisk_{i,t+1}) and optionally (PRisk_{i,t+2}), with the same controls and FE.
**Why needed here:** if future PRisk predicts current uncertainty nearly as well as past PRisk, your timing story is weak. Hassan’s own appendix logic in the lobbying setting is exactly that lag should dominate lead, though they interpret it cautiously because persistence can blur timing.
**Expected pattern:** lead coefficients small and ideally insignificant.
**Pass:** lagged PRisk works; future PRisk does not.
**Fail:** lead looks similar to lag. Then you are mostly seeing persistent firm-quarter conditions.
**Effort:** low.
**Placement:** main text.

### 3. Horse-race: lagged, contemporaneous, and future PRisk together

**Purpose:** force the timing evidence to reveal itself.
**Threat addressed:** persistence, same-call contamination.
**Specification:**
[
Uncertainty_{i,t} = \beta_{-1}PRisk_{i,t-1} + \beta_0PRisk_{i,t} + \beta_{+1}PRisk_{i,t+1} + \Gamma Controls + \alpha_i + \tau_t + \varepsilon_{i,t}
]
**Why needed here:** in your paper, contemporaneous PRisk will probably remain strong because of shared-source issues. The question is whether (PRisk_{t-1}) retains incremental power once that is acknowledged.
**Expected pattern:** (\beta_0) may be strong; (\beta_{-1}>0); (\beta_{+1}) weak.
**Pass:** lag survives and lead is weak.
**Fail:** contemporaneous absorbs everything and lag vanishes. Then the clean thesis is much weaker and must be downgraded to descriptive contemporaneous association.
**Effort:** low-medium, because multicollinearity may widen SEs.
**Placement:** main text.

### 4. Stronger time fixed effects and explicit clustering

**Purpose:** absorb quarter-level common shocks and make inference defensible.
**Threat addressed:** omitted common shocks; weak inference.
**Specification:** firm FE + year-quarter FE as baseline; appendix with 2-digit SIC × year-quarter FE if feasible. Explicitly report firm clustering, and add two-way firm × quarter clustering if your software makes it easy.
**Why needed here:** your current tables show 17 time periods, so you are currently absorbing annual time effects, not quarter-level shocks. That is not enough for political risk or call language.  Hassan show that sector × time controls do not absorb most firm-level PRisk variation, so this robustness is worth doing and may well leave signal intact.
**Expected pattern:** coefficients attenuate somewhat, but not collapse.
**Pass:** sign and material significance survive.
**Fail:** coefficient dies under quarter-level shocks.
**Effort:** medium.
**Placement:** quarter FE in main text; industry × quarter FE in appendix unless it materially changes the story.

### 5. Add direct bad-news controls: earnings surprise and pre-call stock return

**Purpose:** answer “this is just bad times / bad news.”
**Threat addressed:** bad-news and negativity confounding.
**Specification:** add earnings surprise and pre-call return to your current linguistic controls.
**Why needed here:** Hassan explicitly use political sentiment, overall sentiment, pre-call stock returns, and earnings surprise to address the concern that firms are simply blaming politics when performance is bad. Their core coefficients are little changed. You should borrow that logic.
**Expected pattern:** PRisk coefficient remains positive with modest attenuation at most.
**Pass:** little change in PRisk beta.
**Fail:** large shrinkage, especially in lagged models.
**Effort:** low.
**Placement:** main text.

### 6. Overlap-exclusion test

**Purpose:** address the mechanical overlap objection directly.
**Threat addressed:** dictionary overlap, same-language contamination.
**Specification:** rerun the main lagged models after excluding from your uncertainty measure the exact Hassan-style risk/uncertainty conditioning words and any LM uncertainty words that overlap with negative tone. Dzielinski show that in their setting uncertainty-negative overlap is small, but that is not a substitute for checking your own application.
**Why needed here:** this paper is unusually exposed to “you are counting the same words twice.”
**Expected pattern:** smaller coefficient, same sign, still meaningful.
**Pass:** lagged effect survives after exclusions.

**Effort:** low-medium.

### 7. Top-word dominance audit

**Purpose:** verify that one or two high-frequency uncertainty words are not driving everything.

## SECTION 8 — LITERATURE POSITIONING MEMO

### 1. Hassan et al. on firm-level political risk

Hassan et al. construct a firm-level political risk measure from earnings-call transcripts by identifying political bigrams that occur near risk/uncertainty language, and they validate that measure using investment, hiring, lobbying, donations, and volatility outcomes. They are explicit that the baseline evidence is associational, that the measure captures perceived risk discussed on calls, and that causal identification is a separate task. Your paper should borrow all three things from them: the measure, the caution, and the idea that political risk has economically meaningful firm-level variation.

What you add is narrower and should be stated that way: you examine **managerial communication as an outcome**, not real activity, lobbying, or asset-pricing sensitivity. More specifically, you ask whether a validated firm-level political risk measure predicts subsequent uncertainty language in earnings calls. Do not claim you are extending Hassan into causal identification or measuring “actual” political risk better than they do.

### 2. Dzielinski / Wagner / Zeckhauser on clarity / uncertainty speech

Dzielinski, Wagner, and Zeckhauser use LM uncertainty words in earnings calls to measure CEO clarity, defined as the stable style component of uncertainty language in answers after controlling for presentations, analyst-question uncertainty, and time-varying firm conditions. Their key design insight is that presentation and Q&A serve different purposes: presentation is more scripted and firm-level, Q&A more improvised and manager-level.

Your paper borrows the uncertainty-language construct, the presentation/Q&A logic, and the use of analyst-question uncertainty and presentation uncertainty as controls. What you add is **not** a new style measure. You are not decomposing stable manager effects from residual call-by-call deviations. So do not overstate novelty here. The correct positioning is that Dzielinski helps justify your controls and your interpretation of the split, but your contribution is about political risk and communication, not style per se.

### 3. Conference-call informativeness literature

The conference-call literature treats earnings calls as economically important disclosure events. Dzielinski explicitly cite Matsumoto, Pronk, and Roelofsen (2011), Mayew and Venkatachalam (2012), and Brown, Call, Clement, and Sharp (2015) to establish that calls provide incremental information and are highly valued by analysts. They also note that discussion/Q&A is often more informative than prepared remarks.

What your paper adds to this stream is not another general claim that calls matter. It is a narrower claim: when firms face more political risk, their subsequent managerial call language becomes more uncertainty-laden. That is interesting because it places political risk inside a central disclosure venue, not just in investment or lobbying regressions.

### 4. Disclosure / readability / linguistic uncertainty literature

This literature shows that how information is communicated matters. Dzielinski place themselves relative to Loughran and McDonald (2011, 2014, 2016), Rennekamp (2012), You and Zhang (2009), Hwang and Kim (2017), and Demers and Vega (2011), among others, to argue that processing ease and linguistic form affect market reactions.

You borrow the LM uncertainty dictionary and the general premise that textual uncertainty is economically meaningful. Your addition is to link that linguistic uncertainty directly to firm-level political risk. Avoid claiming novelty in dictionary construction, text methods, or the basic idea that uncertainty words matter. That is established terrain.

### 5. Manager style literature

The manager-style literature, starting from Bertrand and Schoar (2003), asks whether persistent manager-specific characteristics shape corporate outcomes. Dzielinski also cite Davis, Ge, Matsumoto, and Zhang (2015), Lee (2016), Adams, Keloharju, and Knüpfer (2018), Green, Jame, and Lock (2019), and Gow, Kaplan, Larcker, and Zakolyukina (2016) to show that speech and disclosure can contain stable manager-specific components.

Your paper should cite this literature carefully but stay out of the contribution race. You are not estimating a stable manager effect and then linking that effect to outcomes. You are using call splits and controls inspired by this literature, but your contribution is not “manager style.”

### 6. Political risk and corporate outcomes literature

The political-risk literature studies how policy uncertainty and political shocks matter for firms, investment, hiring, volatility, and lobbying. Hassan et al. position themselves against aggregate policy uncertainty work and show that firm-level political risk contains large idiosyncratic variation with real economic content.

Your paper’s place here is straightforward: instead of another real-outcomes paper, you study a **communication outcome**. That is enough. Do not say you uncover a new transmission mechanism into “expectations” unless you are comfortable defending speech uncertainty as a direct measure of expectations. Right now, the safer wording is “communicated uncertainty” or “uncertainty language.”

## SECTION 10 — WRITING THE THESIS STORY

The clean narrative arc is this.

Political risk is not just a macro or lobbying story. Hassan et al. show that it varies heavily at the firm level and that this variation has real economic content. If that is true, it should also show up in how managers communicate about firm prospects in one of the most important disclosure settings they face: the earnings call.

Why should political risk show up in managerial speech? Because political risk raises the variance and ambiguity of the environment firms face: regulation, budgets, procurement, taxes, litigation, and government action all make outlook statements harder to phrase confidently. Hassan’s framework is explicitly about political discussion linked to risk/uncertainty language in conference calls. Your question is whether that political risk predicts later uncertainty in managers’ own language.

Why does speech uncertainty matter? Because conference calls are not random chatter. The conference-call literature treats them as a key disclosure setting, and Dzielinski show that persistent uncertainty language in managers’ answers is economically meaningful and distinct from ordinary firm uncertainty.

Why this is not merely a word-count curiosity: both source papers establish that these texts are economically consequential. Hassan show that transcript-based political risk relates to real firm outcomes and lobbying; Dzielinski show that uncertainty language in calls affects how markets process information. Your thesis sits at the intersection: it asks whether political risk shows up in communicated uncertainty.

Why the design is limited but still thesis-worthy: you are not identifying causal effects of political risk on beliefs. You are testing whether a validated firm-level political risk measure predicts a meaningful communication outcome in later calls, while being honest about same-source limits and doing the minimum timing and measurement checks. That is a complete Master’s thesis. It does not need a heroic instrument.
