## A. Red-Team Bottom Line

The first-layer audit is a competent, well-structured document that correctly identifies the major econometric weaknesses of the H13.1 suite (multiple testing, reverse causality, missing robustness). However, it contains one material factual error (the claim that Compustat controls and DVs are constant within firm-year groups is wrong for 82% of groups), one verified variable-formula misstatement (TobinsQ), and several evidence gaps where claims are stated as verified but no independent reproduction command is shown. The issue register is well-calibrated on severity for most items, but the ecological-mismatch critique (L-05) is built on factually incorrect premises. The audit also misses the calendar-year-vs-fiscal-year timing mismatch in the PanelOLS time FE, the stale artifact from the March 6 run, and several unused imports beyond the LaTeX table.

**Overall grade for the first audit: PARTIALLY RELIABLE**

**Suite as implemented: SALVAGEABLE WITH MAJOR REVISIONS**

The first audit **mixed both understated and overstated risk**: it overstated the pseudo-replication problem (J1/L-05) by incorrectly claiming financial variables are constant within firm-year, while understating the calendar-year-vs-fiscal-year FE timing mismatch and the TobinsQ formula error.

## N. What a Committee / Referee Would Still Not Know if They Read Only the First Audit

1. **The time FE is misaligned with the DV timing.** The PanelOLS uses calendar year as time FE, but CapexAt and CapexAt_lead are constructed on fiscal-year boundaries. For 24% of observations, these differ. This means the "year" fixed effect does not absorb the confounds most relevant to the DV. This is arguably a more fundamental issue than anything in the first audit's issue register.

## P. Final Red-Team Readiness Statement

**Can the first audit be trusted as a standalone referee-quality document?**
Not entirely. It is a competent audit with correct identification of the major econometric weaknesses (multiple testing, reverse causality, missing robustness), but it contains one material factual error (within-group variation claim) that invalidates a significant portion of its analysis (J1, L-05, and the "collapse to firm-year" recommendation). It also misses the calendar-year vs fiscal-year timing mismatch, which is arguably a more fundamental issue than the duplicate-index problem it focuses on.

**What is its biggest factual weakness?**
The claim in J1 that "Two calls from the same firm in the same year share the same CapexAt, CapexAt_lead, and all Compustat controls -- only the linguistic variables vary." This is wrong for 82% of firm-year groups. The entire L-05 issue and the primary recommendation (collapse to firm-year) are built on this false premise.

**What is its biggest completeness weakness?**
The absence of any discussion of the calendar-year vs fiscal-year timing mismatch. The PanelOLS time FE uses `year` (calendar year from start_date) while the DV and all Compustat controls are constructed on `fyearq_int` (fiscal year from Compustat). This affects 24% of observations and creates misaligned fixed-effect absorption.

**What is its biggest severity/judgment weakness?**
L-05 is marked as "blocks thesis-standard" based on the incorrect pseudo-replication claim. This inflates the urgency of the wrong fix (collapse to firm-year) while the correct fix (align time FE to fiscal year) is not mentioned.

**What is the single most important missed issue?**
G-01: The calendar-year time FE vs fiscal-year DV timing mismatch (new issue RT-03). This is a genuine identification concern because the year fixed effects do not absorb the time-varying confounds most relevant to the fiscal-year-based outcome variable.

**What is the single most misleading claim?**
The J1 characterization of the data structure, which cascades into an incorrect effective-sample-size argument, an incorrect thesis-blocking severity, and an incorrect primary recommendation.

**What should a thesis committee believe after reading this red-team review?**
The H13.1 suite has correct regression mechanics but produces overwhelmingly null results (3/24 significant at chance levels). The first-layer audit correctly identifies this and the major econometric weaknesses. However, the first audit's analysis of the duplicate-index issue is built on a factual error about within-group variation, and it misses a more important timing-alignment issue. The committee should:
1. Trust the first audit's verdict that H13.1 is "SALVAGEABLE WITH MAJOR REVISIONS."
2. Prioritize fixing the time FE to use fiscal year (fyearq_int) rather than calendar year.
3. Disregard the "collapse to firm-year" recommendation as currently formulated (it would destroy legitimate variation); instead, align the PanelOLS time index to fiscal year.
4. Accept that even after fixes, the 3/24 significance pattern is unlikely to survive multiple-testing correction.
5. Correct the TobinsQ formula in the variable dictionary.
