## G. Outliers, Missing Data, Scaling, and Model-Specific Data Rules

### G.1 Moderator Transformation Rationale

Raw TNIC3TSIMM has heavy right skew (2.81) and extreme range [1, 143]. The log transform reduces skewness to 1.06, improving the linear-interaction assumption. Z-scoring centers at zero and scales to unit variance for interpretability: a one-SD increase in log(TSIMM) maps to a one-unit change in the moderator.

### G.2 TSIMM = 1 Handling

5.0% of Main sample calls have TSIMM = 1 (no TNIC3 peers). These are NOT dropped; log(1) = 0 is valid, producing z = -0.9321. These firms form a mass point at the moderator floor. The interaction effect for these firms is partially identified but may be unstable. No sensitivity analysis excluding TSIMM=1 firms is implemented.

### G.3 Inf/NaN Handling

- `prepare_regression_data()` line 258: `df.replace([np.inf, -np.inf], np.nan)` -- all infinities converted to NaN before complete-case deletion.
- TSIMM is always >= 1 in the TNIC data, so `log(TSIMM) >= 0` -- no log-of-zero or negative-log risk.
- Missing TSIMM (1.6% of Main sample) produces NaN in z_log_tnic3tsimm and interaction, causing complete-case dropout.

### G.4 No Additional Winsorization

The moderator (z_log_tnic3tsimm) is NOT winsorized -- neither before nor after the z-score transformation. The max z-score of 4.50 represents a firm with very high product similarity. Whether this constitutes an outlier is debatable. Robustness to trimming at |z| > 3 is not tested.

### G.5 Summary Statistics Scope

Summary statistics (`summary_stats.csv`) are computed on the full Main sample (88,205 calls) AFTER the Industry/Utility filter but BEFORE complete-case deletion and TNIC filtering. The z(log(TSIMM)) shows N=86,807 (TNIC-matched subset of Main). These statistics do NOT exactly match the regression sample (76,239 for Col 1, 72,618 for Col 2).

## K. Corporate Finance Thesis Referee Assessment

### K1. Identification

**Assessment: Weak for causal claims; adequate for descriptive moderation**

The suite tests whether a cross-sectional moderator (product-market similarity) modifies a descriptive association (uncertainty language and cash holdings). There is no exogenous variation in either the IV or the moderator. The interaction b3 = 0.009 (p = 0.085) in Col 1 is at best suggestive.

The identification strategy relies on conditional correlations within Industry+FY FE. With only 8.3% within-firm variation in the moderator, the interaction is primarily identified from between-firm differences in product-market similarity -- firms that are permanently in more crowded markets are compared to firms in less crowded markets. This is inherently confounded by all other between-firm differences correlated with product-market structure (industry dynamics, firm strategy, management quality).

**No instrumental variable, no exogenous shock, no RDD.** Standard for exploratory moderation analysis in corporate finance, but the reader should understand this is a conditional correlation, not a causal estimate.

### K2. Inference

**Assessment: Marginal significance is fragile and potentially overstated**

1. **The headline result (b3 p=0.085) does not meet the conventional 5% threshold.** The 10% threshold is occasionally used in finance but is increasingly frowned upon (Harvey, Liu, and Zhu 2016). Reporting this as a "significant moderation effect" would be misleading.

2. **Multicollinearity:** Corr(moderator, interaction) = 0.93. This is a textbook case of the multicollinearity problem in moderation analysis. The interaction SE (0.0052) may be inflated by 2-3x compared to a properly orthogonalized specification. Mean-centering the IV before forming the interaction would reduce this correlation substantially (the residual Corr would be ~0.11, the Corr(IV, moderator) value).

3. **Multiple testing:** If this is the "secondary moderator" from a moderation memo, how many moderators were tested? If TSIMM was selected after observing results from a primary moderator (or from data mining across moderator candidates), the effective p-value is higher than reported.

4. **Moulton problem:** The moderator varies at firm-year level but is repeated across ~3.85 calls per firm-year. Firm-clustered SEs account for within-firm correlation but the precision for the interaction coefficient specifically may be overstated. A firm-year-level regression would be the definitive test.

5. **One-tailed test on the IV:** The one-tailed p=0.069 for the main IV is reported with a star (*) at the 10% level. This is legitimate given the directional hypothesis, but combined with the two-tailed 10% interaction, the table gives a misleading impression of robust significance.

### K3. Robustness

**Assessment: No robustness checks implemented -- this is a single-specification result**

A referee would expect at minimum:
- Firm-year collapse (to address Moulton)
- Mean-centering the IV before forming interaction (to address multicollinearity)
- Alternative moderator (TNIC3HHI, which is available in the same data file)
- Subsample splits: high vs low TSIMM (above/below median)
- Trimming TSIMM=1 firms (5% mass point at the moderator floor)
- Replication of the parent effect on the H1.1 sample (to confirm the main effect exists in this sample before testing moderation)
- Firm FE specification (even if expected to fail, the comparison is informative)

**None of these are implemented.** A marginal result (p=0.085) with zero robustness checks would not survive peer review.

### K4. Interpretation / Economic Magnitude

**Assessment: Not discussed; difficult to interpret given the specification**

The interaction coefficient b3 = 0.0090 means: a one-SD increase in log(TSIMM) (from a moderately competitive firm to a highly competitive firm) is associated with a 0.9 percentage-point increase in the marginal effect of Manager_Pres_Uncertainty_pct on CashHoldings. Given that the mean CashHoldings = 0.17 (17% of assets), this is a ~5% relative effect on the margin.

However, because the IV is not mean-centered, the "direct effect" of the moderator (b2 = 0.028) represents the moderator effect when Manager_Pres_Uncertainty_pct = 0, which is at the boundary of the IV distribution (minimum is 0). The decomposition into "direct moderator effect" and "interaction effect" is therefore not economically interpretable without centering.

### K5. Academic Integrity

**Assessment: No fabrication detected; documentation is transparent about limitations**

- All coefficients, p-values, and N counts verified against output artifacts.
- The run_manifest.json records input hashes, enabling full reproducibility.
- The report honestly labels the interaction as marginal (stars at 10% level).
- The LaTeX table notes correctly distinguish one-tailed (IV) from two-tailed (interaction) tests.

**One concern:** The LaTeX table shows the IV with a * (one-tailed 10%) and the interaction with a * (two-tailed 10%), using the same star symbol for different test types. A careful reader will catch the footnote distinction, but a casual reader may be misled. Best practice would be to use different notation or to report only two-tailed p-values throughout.

### K6. Relation to Literature

The TNIC total similarity measure (TSIMM) has been used in the product-market competition literature (Hoberg & Phillips JPE 2016, Hoberg & Phillips RFS 2010). The precautionary savings motivation for cash holdings in competitive markets is well-established (Haushalter, Klasa, and Maxwell 2007; Fresard JFE 2010).

The direct effect of TSIMM on CashHoldings (b = 0.028, p < 0.001) is consistent with the competitive-threat-precautionary-savings channel and is well-supported in the literature. This is actually a more interesting finding than the interaction, but it is treated as a control rather than a result.

The interaction (does TSIMM moderate the effect of managerial uncertainty language?) is novel but weakly supported by theory. Why would speech uncertainty predict cash holdings more strongly for firms in competitive markets? The mechanism is unclear. Is it that competitive pressure makes managers more uncertain (a main effect, not an interaction)? Or that markets punish uncertain speech more when competition is fierce (a capital market channel, not a cash-holdings channel)?

### K7. Overall Assessment

**Verdict: Not thesis-ready in current form. Marginal evidence with no robustness, severe multicollinearity, and unclear mechanism.**

The suite produces one marginally significant interaction (p=0.085) that vanishes in the lead specification (p=0.94). This is insufficient to support a claim that product-market similarity moderates the uncertainty-cash relationship. The direct moderator effect (TSIMM -> CashHoldings) is strong and well-established, but that is not the suite's research question.

To become thesis-ready, the suite needs:
1. Mean-center the IV before interaction (reduces multicollinearity from r=0.93 to r~0.11)
2. Firm-year collapse robustness
3. At least one alternative moderator or subsample split
4. Confirmation that the parent main effect holds in the H1.1 sample
5. Honest framing: if the interaction remains marginal after fixes, discuss it as suggestive evidence, not a confirmed finding
