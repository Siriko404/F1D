# Final Proposal Draft

## Title

**Real-Time Abnormal CEO Q&A Hedging and Subsequent Downside Operating Shortfalls**

## One-sentence research question

Do unusually hedge-heavy CEO answers in earnings-call Q&A, measured relative to the CEO’s own prior answer style and contemporaneous call conditions using only information available at the time of the call, predict subsequent downside operating shortfalls?

## Core contribution

This paper studies whether **abnormal CEO Q&A hedging** contains incremental predictive information about future downside operating outcomes. The design is motivated by the conference-call decomposition in *CEO Clarity*, which shows that persistent CEO answer-language style can be separated from presentation language and contemporaneous call conditions, but also shows that the paper’s residual answer-uncertainty component is not the validated economic object in its main results. The proposed paper therefore reframes the forecast variable narrowly and defensibly: not as “latent firm uncertainty,” but as a **real-time abnormal-answer hedging signal** extracted from unscripted CEO Q&A.  

## Main claim

The paper will make a **predictive**, not causal, claim:

> Within firms, unusually hedge-heavy CEO answers during earnings-call Q&A predict a higher likelihood and greater severity of subsequent downside operating shortfalls.

The paper will **not** claim that it identifies latent firm uncertainty or a structural state variable.

---

## Hypotheses

### H1. Predictive downside hypothesis

**H1a.** Higher real-time abnormal CEO Q&A hedging at quarter (t) predicts a greater probability of at least one negative operating-cash-flow quarter over the next four fiscal quarters.

**H1b.** Higher real-time abnormal CEO Q&A hedging at quarter (t) predicts a lower worst-quarter operating cash flow over the next four fiscal quarters.

**H1c.** Higher real-time abnormal CEO Q&A hedging at quarter (t) predicts lower average operating cash flow over the next four fiscal quarters.

### H2. Incremental information hypothesis

The predictive association between abnormal CEO Q&A hedging and subsequent downside operating outcomes survives controls for:

1. current-quarter disclosed fundamentals,
2. prepared-remarks hedging,
3. analyst-question hedging and negativity,
4. tone-based call measures, and
5. alternative Q&A friction measures such as nonanswers and scriptedness.

### H3. Near-term information-environment mechanism

Higher abnormal CEO Q&A hedging predicts greater **post-call analyst forecast dispersion**, consistent with the idea that hedge-heavy unscripted answers leave analysts with more unresolved uncertainty immediately after the call.

### H4. Beyond short-run information processing

The predictive association between abnormal CEO Q&A hedging and later downside operating outcomes remains after controlling for post-call analyst forecast dispersion, indicating that the signal is not merely a short-run information-environment effect.

### H5. Interpretation discipline hypothesis

If the abnormal-hedging signal is just a proxy for other call frictions, its predictive coefficient should attenuate materially once horse-race controls for scriptedness, nonanswers, answer avoidance, and question hostility are included. If it remains significant, that strengthens the interpretation of abnormal hedging as a distinct predictive communication signal.

---

## Conceptual framing

The paper’s main explanatory variable is **not** “firm uncertainty.” The source paper defines the stable component of CEO answer uncertainty as a CEO fixed effect, `ClarityCEO = -γ_i`, and defines the unexplained component as the residual from that decomposition. It explicitly states that the residual is the part of answer uncertainty not explained by the included controls, and its own main results show that persistent clarity matters for market reactions whereas `UncResCEO` explains little of those immediate responses.  

Accordingly, this proposal uses the narrower term:

**Abnormal CEO Q&A hedging**
= the real-time unexplained component of hedge-like uncertainty-word use in CEO answers, conditional on the CEO’s prior answer style, prepared remarks, analyst questions, tone, and current disclosed conditions.

That wording is the strongest defensible construct statement.

---

## Data and sample

The paper uses U.S. quarterly earnings-call transcripts with identifiable CEO participation, split into prepared remarks and Q&A. The source paper shows that this setting is especially useful because the same CEO speaks in a scripted presentation and then in more improvised answers on the same firm-date, which helps separate persistent style from contemporaneous conditions. It also documents that answer-language patterns travel more with the CEO than presentation-language patterns do.  

The main estimation sample will be structured as a **firm-quarter call panel**. To avoid overlap bias, the main design will use **one focal call per firm-year** or otherwise **non-overlapping annual forward windows**.

## Variable definitions

## 1. Main explanatory variable

### `AbnHedgeCEO_{i,t}`: real-time abnormal CEO Q&A hedging

For firm (i) in call-quarter (t), define:

* `HedgeAnsCEO_{i,t}`: fraction of hedge/uncertainty words in CEO answers during Q&A.
* `HedgePreCEO_{i,t}`: fraction of hedge/uncertainty words in CEO prepared remarks.
* `HedgeQue_{i,t}`: fraction of hedge/uncertainty words in analyst questions.

The linguistic base follows the source paper’s use of Loughran–McDonald uncertainty words in conference calls. The source paper reports that common answer words include “probably,” “could,” and “believe,” and defines answer uncertainty as the fraction of uncertainty words in CEO answers.  

The paper’s main regressor is the **real-time residual** from a recursive first-stage model:

[
HedgeAnsCEO_{i,t}
=================

\alpha_t
+
\widehat{\theta}*{CEO(i),t-1}
+
\beta_1 HedgePreCEO*{i,t}
+
\beta_2 HedgeQue_{i,t}
+
\beta_3 ToneCall_{i,t}
+
\beta_4 Q&AControls_{i,t}
+
\beta_5 CurrentFundamentals_{i,t}
+
u_{i,t}
]

and define:

[
AbnHedgeCEO_{i,t} \equiv \widehat{u}_{i,t}
]

where the CEO-specific expected-style term (\widehat{\theta}_{CEO(i),t-1}) is estimated **recursively using only calls observed up to quarter (t-1)**.

### Recursive construction rule

At each quarter (t):

1. Estimate the first stage using only data available through (t-1).
2. Form the CEO-specific expected answer-hedging benchmark.
3. Predict expected hedging for the current call using contemporaneous controls observed at the time of the call.
4. Define abnormal hedging as actual minus predicted.

This replaces the source paper’s full-sample fixed-effect object in the main predictive design, because the original full-sample construction is not real-time. The source paper’s Equation (4) uses CEO fixed effects to define `ClarityCEO` and residual `UncResCEO`; that motivates the architecture, but the proposal’s forecasting version must be recursive.  

---

## 2. Main dependent variables

### `NegOCFAny4_{i,t}`

Indicator equal to 1 if firm (i) records at least one quarter with negative operating cash flow over quarters (t+1) through (t+4), scaled by average assets.

### `WorstOCF4_{i,t}`

Minimum quarterly operating cash flow over quarters (t+1) through (t+4), scaled by average assets.

### `MeanOCF4_{i,t}`

Average operating cash flow over quarters (t+1) through (t+4), scaled by average assets.

These are the headline outcomes because they are transparent, corporate-finance relevant, and not metric-engineered.

---

## 3. Secondary downside outcome

### `LPM0_OCF4_{i,t}`

Lower partial moment of future OCF relative to a zero benchmark:

[
LPM0_OCF4_{i,t}
===============

\frac{1}{4}
\sum_{h=1}^{4}
\max(0,,-OCF_{i,t+h})^2
]

This is optional and secondary. It is acceptable only because the benchmark is economically meaningful. The paper will **not** use semivariance around the future mean as a headline downside measure.

---

## 4. Mechanism variable

### `PostDispersion_{i,t}`

Analyst forecast dispersion measured shortly after the call, for example the standard deviation of analysts’ next-quarter EPS forecasts in the days following the call.

This is the main nearby information-environment mechanism.

---

## 5. Secondary continuation outcomes

### `SurpriseNext_{i,t}`

Indicator or signed magnitude for a negative earnings surprise in the next reporting period.

### `GuidanceMissNext_{i,t}`

Indicator for missing previously issued guidance in the next reporting period.

These are **not** uncertainty measures. They are secondary bad-news continuation outcomes.

---

## 6. Core controls

### A. Call-language controls

* `HedgePreCEO_{i,t}`: hedge-word fraction in CEO prepared remarks.
* `HedgeQue_{i,t}`: hedge-word fraction in analyst questions.
* `ToneCall_{i,t}`: overall call negativity / tone.
* `ToneChange_{i,t}`: change in negativity relative to prior call.
* `WordsCall_{i,t}`: total call word count.
* `NumCall_{i,t}`: numerical-density measure.
* `ComplexCall_{i,t}`: average sentence length or related complexity proxy.

The source paper uses analogous controls, including presentation uncertainty, analyst-question uncertainty, negativity, word count, numerical content, and complexity proxies, in its market-response specifications.  

### B. Alternative Q&A friction controls

* `Scriptedness_{i,t}`: similarity of answers to prepared language / lack of spontaneity proxy.
* `NonAnswer_{i,t}`: fraction of answers classified as nonanswers.
* `Avoidance_{i,t}`: answer-avoidance measure.
* `QuestionNegativity_{i,t}`: negativity or hostility of analyst questions.
* `QuestionDifficulty_{i,t}`: proxy for questioning pressure or complexity.

### C. Current-quarter disclosed fundamentals

Measured using information already released with or by the current call:

* current OCF / assets,
* revenue growth,
* gross margin or operating margin,
* accruals or working-capital pressure,
* cash holdings,
* leverage,
* recent return volatility,
* earnings surprise,
* current guidance indicator and guidance characteristics,
* size,
* market-to-book / Tobin’s Q,
* industry demand controls where available.

These controls are critical because the call occurs after current-quarter results are known.

## Model specifications

## Model 1. Recursive first-stage construction of abnormal hedging

This is the defining model for the main explanatory variable.

[
HedgeAnsCEO_{i,t}
=================

\alpha_t
+
\widehat{\theta}*{CEO(i),t-1}
+
\beta_1 HedgePreCEO*{i,t}
+
\beta_2 HedgeQue_{i,t}
+
\beta_3 ToneCall_{i,t}
+
\beta_4 Q&AControls_{i,t}
+
\beta_5 CurrentFundamentals_{i,t}
+
u_{i,t}
]

[
AbnHedgeCEO_{i,t} = \widehat{u}_{i,t}
]

### Notes

* `\widehat{\theta}_{CEO(i),t-1}` is estimated using only prior calls.
* The model is estimated in an expanding window.
* Minimum-history requirements and shrinkage rules will be specified ex ante.
* The full-sample fixed-effect version, corresponding conceptually to the source paper’s Equation (4), appears only in the appendix as a non-real-time benchmark. The source paper defines `ClarityCEO` as the negative CEO fixed effect and `UncResCEO` as the residual from this decomposition.

[
Y_{i,t\rightarrow t+4}

\alpha

* Linear probability model for `NegOCFAny4`, with logit/probit as robustness.
* OLS for `WorstOCF4` and `MeanOCF4`.

### Prediction

[
\beta > 0 \text{ for } NegOCFAny4
]
[
\beta < 0 \text{ for } WorstOCF4, MeanOCF4

## Model 6. Secondary turnover/change design

Because persistent style is absorbed by firm fixed effects in the main predictive specification, any “incremental-to-style” claim must be handled separately. The appropriate supplemental design is a turnover or CEO-change specification:

[
\Delta Y_{j}
============

\alpha
+
\beta , \Delta StyleHedge_{j}
+
\Gamma' \Delta Controls_{j}
+
\phi_{industry(j)}
+
\varepsilon_j
]

This is a supplement, not the headline design. The source paper’s turnover analysis is the right template for examining persistent style changes, and it shows that greater clarity is associated with higher Tobin’s Q and more favorable analyst recommendations but not higher operating performance.

## Inference plan

## Baseline inference

* Two-way clustered standard errors by **firm** and **calendar quarter/event quarter**.

## Overlap control

* Main analysis uses **non-overlapping annual forward windows** or **one call per firm-year**.

## Generated-regressor robustness

Because the main explanatory variable is estimated in a first stage, robustness will re-estimate the recursive first stage inside the resampling loop. This is needed because the source-paper-style decomposition generates first-stage objects (`ClarityCEO`, `UncResCEO`) rather than directly observed regressors.  

## Resampling robustness

* Firm-level block bootstrap or wild-cluster bootstrap, with the recursive first stage rebuilt in each draw.

## Identification statement

The paper identifies a **within-firm conditional predictive association**. It does not identify:

* a causal effect of CEO language on future fundamentals,
* a structural latent uncertainty factor,
* or a purified measure of firm uncertainty.

The interpretation is deliberately narrower:

> unusually hedge-heavy unscripted CEO answers predict later downside operating shortfalls, even after rich controls for current conditions and alternative Q&A frictions.

That is the appropriate claim given the source paper’s decomposition and evidence. The source paper itself shows that its residual answer-uncertainty component explains little of immediate market reaction, whereas persistent clarity carries the main economic effects.

## What the paper will and will not say

### The paper will say

* The main signal is **real-time abnormal CEO Q&A hedging**.
* The design is **predictive**.
* The outcomes are **transparent downside operating outcomes**.
* The signal survives or fails against explicit horse races with nonanswers, scriptedness, tone, and question hostility.

### The paper will not say

* The regressor measures latent firm uncertainty.
* The source paper already validated the residual as an operating-risk construct.
* The evidence is causal.
* Guidance misses or negative surprises are uncertainty mechanisms.

## Short proposal abstract

This paper tests whether unusually hedge-heavy CEO answers during earnings-call Q&A predict subsequent downside operating shortfalls. The main explanatory variable is a **real-time abnormal CEO Q&A hedging measure**, constructed recursively from conference-call language using only information available at the time of the call. The measure captures the unexplained component of CEO answer hedging after controlling for prepared-remarks hedging, analyst-question hedging, tone, call structure, and current disclosed fundamentals. The paper’s main outcomes are transparent downside operating measures: the incidence of at least one negative operating-cash-flow quarter over the following year, the worst-quarter operating cash flow, and average operating cash flow. The empirical design emphasizes within-firm predictive regressions with firm and calendar-quarter fixed effects, non-overlapping horizons, and horse races against alternative Q&A friction measures such as scriptedness, nonanswers, answer avoidance, and question hostility. The paper’s contribution is narrow but potentially important: it shows whether abnormal hedging in unscripted CEO answers is a distinct forward-looking signal of later operating downside, without claiming to recover a structural measure of latent firm uncertainty.
