## Referee report (hard-nosed, fair): H10 / H_TT “Tone at the Top” transmission

### What you claim (as written)

You test whether CEO uncertainty language “transmits” to other managers using two designs: (i) a call-level model where a *predetermined* “CEO realtime style” (rolling 4-call EB-shrunk) predicts CFO Q&A uncertainty, and (ii) a within-call “Granger-style” model where CEO *prior* Q&A uncertainty predicts non-CEO manager uncertainty later in the same call, with Call FE + Speaker FE. 

The headline results in Table 1 are:

* **M1 (call-level)**: CEO Style is positive and significant in **Main**, not in Finance/Utility (e.g., 0.0139*** in Main). 
* **M2 (turn-level)**: CEO Prior Q&A Unc is positive and highly significant across samples (e.g., 0.0426*** Main; 0.0309*** Finance; 0.0243*** Utility). 

---

## Recommendation

**Major revision** before this can be sold as “transmission/contagion.” Right now, the evidence is consistent with *shared within-call shocks and sequencing* just as much as it is with genuine mimicry.

---

## Strengths (what’s genuinely solid)

1. **Clear two-level empirical framing** (trait-ish cross-call vs within-call dynamics) and good implementation transparency (construction, FE structure, merging prior turns). 
2. **Within-call FE strategy is directionally right**: Call FE removes all call-constant confounds; Speaker FE removes stable speaker style. 
3. **Strict “prior turns only” implementation** (no exact matches) is the correct baseline for any temporal ordering claim. 

Those are the bones of a credible design.

---

## Major concerns (these are the reasons a top-journal referee would not buy “transmission” yet)

### 1) M2 is extremely vulnerable to **within-call time trends / question difficulty**, not just “CEO influence”

Your key regressor is an **expanding mean of CEO uncertainty up to that point**. 
That object is mechanically correlated with *where you are in the call* and with the *mix of questions already asked*. If later questions are systematically harder/more uncertain and also more likely to be delegated to other managers, you will get a positive β even with **zero mimicry**.

**What would convince me you have transmission rather than sequencing:**

* Replace expanding mean with **local lags**: last CEO turn uncertainty; mean of last k CEO turns (k=1,2,3); exponentially decayed lag.
* Add **turn index / elapsed-time controls** (and ideally flexible splines) within call.
* Control for **analyst question uncertainty** (text features of the immediately preceding analyst turn), or even better: **Analyst-turn fixed effects** (or question-topic FE).
  If β survives these, your “transmission” story becomes materially stronger.

### 2) Your causal language (“Granger-style causality”) is too strong for the assumptions you actually have

You explicitly assume ordering is “quasi-random” because analysts drive it. 
That’s exactly the problem: analyst order is *strategic* and correlated with news, complexity, controversy, and who answers. Call FE does not solve **time-varying within-call confounds**.

**Minimum fix:** soften claims to “within-call association consistent with transmission” until you run the time-trend/question-difficulty robustness above.

### 3) M1 likely conditions on **post-treatment variables** (“bad controls” risk)

In M1 you include same-call **CEO Q&A uncertainty** and **CEO presentation uncertainty** as controls. 
If the mechanism is “CEO style → CEO uncertainty in this call → CFO uncertainty,” then controlling for CEO’s same-call uncertainty partially *blocks the causal channel* and can bias estimates (sign and magnitude ambiguous). It also opens collider paths if an unobserved “call difficulty” shock raises both CEO and CFO uncertainty.

**What I would require:**

* Report M1 **with and without** these CEO same-call controls (and interpret differences as mechanism vs conditioning bias).
* If you keep them, explicitly frame M1’s β as “style effect **holding constant CEO’s contemporaneous uncertainty content**,” which is a narrower—and more defensible—estimand.

### 4) Inference / clustering: Utility sample is too small to trust conventional clustered SE

Utility M1 has **62 firms** and 1,403 observations. 
Even Utility M2 ultimately relies on relatively few higher-level clusters (depending on CEO count). Two-way clustered SE can be unreliable with small cluster counts.

**What I would require:**

* **Wild cluster bootstrap** p-values for Utility (and arguably Finance).
* For M2 specifically, consider clustering by **call** (or multi-way including call), since the data generating process is “turns within calls.” Firm clustering is not a substitute for call clustering in a within-call dynamic model.

### 5) Measurement & classification error could be first-order here (and not classical)

Your role identification uses regex patterns for CFO and a very broad “manager” pattern. 
Misclassification that is correlated with uncertainty (e.g., certain roles speak in harder parts of calls) can generate spurious “transmission.” Also, `speaker_name` is often not a stable unique identifier across firms/years; Speaker FE can be noisy if names collide.

**What I would require:**

* Manual validation on a stratified sample: precision/recall for CEO/CFO/non-CEO manager tags.
* Sensitivity: run M2 on **CFO-only**, then on **non-CFO managers**, then on **top executives (COO/President)** separately.

### 6) Economic significance is not established

Your coefficients are on **IHS(transformed percent)**. 
Given huge N, statistical significance is cheap. You need to translate to something interpretable:

* Move from p-values to: “from 25th→75th percentile CEO prior uncertainty, predicted manager uncertainty changes by X bps (or X% of SD).”
* Plot predicted effects.

---

## “Must-run” robustness suite (if you do only 6 things, do these)

1. **Local lag** instead of expanding mean (last CEO turn; last 2 CEO turns).
2. Add **turn index / elapsed time** controls (flexible).
3. Add **analyst question uncertainty** controls (immediate prior analyst turn).
4. **Placebo ordering tests**: future CEO uncertainty should not predict current manager uncertainty (lead test).
5. **Permutation within call**: shuffle turn order within call; your β should go to ~0.
6. **Bootstrap inference** (wild cluster) for Utility; cluster-by-call for M2.

If β persists across these, your “tone-at-the-top transmission” claim becomes substantially harder to dismiss.

---

## Bottom line

* The **pattern** (M1 only in Main; M2 positive everywhere) is interesting and potentially publishable. 
* But as a referee, I would currently interpret M2 as: **CEO prior uncertainty is a proxy for evolving within-call difficulty/topic mix**, not necessarily behavioral contagion.
* You’re close: a targeted robustness battery can convert this from “plausible association” to “credible transmission evidence.”
