## Hypothesis statement

A clean publication-style version is:

**H1.** Higher earnings-call language uncertainty is associated with lower market liquidity, measured by a wider bid-ask spread, around the conference call.

If you want it more explicit:

**H1.** Firms whose earnings conference calls exhibit greater managerial language uncertainty experience a larger increase in bid-ask spreads in the days surrounding the call.

This framing fits the literature well because conference calls are part of the firm’s information environment, and spread-based measures are commonly used to capture information asymmetry and liquidity around disclosure events. Prior work studies conference calls and information asymmetry directly, and related conference-call papers use short-window changes in bid-ask spreads around the call as an outcome. ([ScienceDirect][1])

---

## Null and alternative

Use:

[
H_0:\beta \le 0
]

[
H_1:\beta > 0
]

If you prefer a two-sided test:

[
H_0:\beta = 0
]

[
H_1:\beta \ne 0
]

But for your theory, the **one-sided directional prediction** is more natural.

## Preferred main specification

The strongest baseline is an **event-window change** in spread around the call:

[
\Delta Spread_{i,q}
===================

\alpha
+
\beta , Uncertainty_{i,q}
+
\gamma' Controls_{i,q}
+
\eta_i
+
\tau_t
+
\varepsilon_{i,q}
]

where

[
\Delta Spread_{i,q}
===================

## \overline{Spread}_{i,q,[+1,+3]}

\overline{Spread}_{i,q,[-3,-1]}
]

and

[
Spread_{i,q,d}
==============

\frac{Ask_{i,q,d}-Bid_{i,q,d}}
{\left(Ask_{i,q,d}+Bid_{i,q,d}\right)/2}
]

### Interpretation

* (i) = firm
* (q) = earnings-call quarter
* (\Delta Spread) = post-call minus pre-call average relative bid-ask spread
* (Uncertainty) = LM-based language uncertainty from the earnings call
* (\eta_i) = firm fixed effects
* (\tau_t) = time fixed effects, typically quarter or year-quarter fixed effects

This is the cleanest way to align the dependent variable with the disclosure event. It is also close to how prior conference-call studies define change in bid-ask spread around the call. ([Harvard Kennedy School][2])

## Recommended wording for the empirical section

You can write it like this:

> We examine whether managerial language uncertainty in earnings conference calls is associated with lower market liquidity around the disclosure event. Specifically, we regress the change in the firm’s relative bid-ask spread from the pre-call window ([-3,-1]) to the post-call window ([+1,+3]) on call-level language uncertainty, controlling for firm characteristics and including firm and time fixed effects.

That is methodologically clean and does **not** overclaim causality.

---

## Controls

A compact standard control vector is:

[
Controls_{i,q}
==============

{
Size,\ Price,\ Turnover,\ ReturnVolatility,\ PreCallSpread,\ AbsSurprise
}
]

You can add analyst coverage, institutional ownership, and tone if available. Spread-based conference-call papers commonly control for baseline information environment and trading frictions in this spirit. ([www-2.rotman.utoronto.ca][3])

---

## What I would call the test

Use this label:

**Event-window panel test of liquidity around earnings conference calls**

Do **not** call it a classic event study unless you explicitly model **abnormal** spreads relative to an expected benchmark.
